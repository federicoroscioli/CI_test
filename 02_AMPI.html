1.1 Scope of the Analysis

The dataset includes three indicators — income, education, and environment — measured for a set of countries. The goal is to construct a composite index that aggregates these dimensions into a single score reflecting overall performance. The index must be positive (i.e. multidimensional development) and we assume that all 3 indicators are measured in a way that higher values indicate better performance (e.g., higher income, better education outcomes, cleaner environment).

2. Normalization Comparison

2.1 Rationale

Raw indicators are measured on different scales and units, making direct comparison or aggregation meaningless. We therefore normalize each indicator onto a common scale before aggregating.

2.2 Standardization

A common approach is z-score standardization, which transforms each variable to have a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of 1: \[z_i = \frac{x_i - \bar{x}}{\sigma}\]

Where:

  • \(x_i\) is the raw value for country \(i\)
  • \(\bar{x}\) is the mean of the indicator across all countries
  • \(\sigma\) is the standard deviation of the indicator across all countries This method centers the data around zero and rescales it based on variability, but it does not set a fixed range or reference point for interpretation.

2.3 Min-Max Normalization

Another common method is min-max normalization, which rescales the data to a fixed range, typically [0, 1]: \[z_i = \frac{x_i - x_{\min}}{x_{\max} - x_{\min}}\] Where: - \(x_i\) is the raw value for country \(i\) - \(x_{\min}\) is the minimum observed value of the indicator across all countries - \(x_{\max}\) is the maximum observed value of the indicator across all countries This method preserves the relative position of each country within the observed range but can be sensitive to outliers and does not set a meaningful reference point for performance.

2.4 AMPI Normalization: Constrained Min-Max with Goalpost

The normalization uses a constrained min-max approach where: - The goalpost (reference point) is the cross-country median of each indicator — i.e., all countries aim at reaching the median European value. - The normalized score is bounded between 70 (worst performer) and 130 (best performer), yielding a symmetric range of ±30 around the central value of 100.

For each indicator \(x\), the normalized score \(z\) is: \[z_i = 70 + 60 \cdot \frac{x_i - \tilde{x}}{x_{\max} - x_{\min}}\]

Where: - \(x_i\) | Raw value for country \(i\) - \(\tilde{x}\) | Median of the indicator across all countries - \(x_{\max}\) | Maximum observed value - \(x_{\min}\) | Minimum observed value - \(70\) | Floor score (worst performer) - \(130\) | Ceiling score (best performer)

The multiplier \(60\) reflects the total range \([70, 130]\), so that deviations from the median are rescaled proportionally within that interval.

2.5 Comparison of Normalization Methods

Comparing the distributions of the normalized indicators under each method reveals how the choice of normalization affects the relative positioning of countries and the interpretability of scores. The AMPI normalization centers scores around 100 with a fixed range, while standardization centers around 0 without a fixed range, and min-max normalization rescales to [0, 1] based on observed extremes.

Each method has different implications for how we interpret the scores and how sensitive they are to outliers or the distribution of the raw data. The AMPI method, by using a median goalpost and a fixed range, emphasizes relative performance against a central benchmark while allowing for a clear interpretation of scores as “above” or “below” the median. The standardization method emphasizes deviations from the mean in terms of standard deviations, while the min-max method emphasizes relative position within the observed range.

The Coefficient of Variation (CV) measures relative dispersion as the ratio of the standard deviation to the mean: \[CV = \frac{\sigma}{\bar{x}}\]

Comparing CVs before and after normalization helps verify that the transformation preserves the relative variability structure across indicators.

3. Comparison of Aggregation Methods

3.1 Rationale

After normalization, we need to aggregate the multiple indicators into a single composite index. The choice of aggregation method affects how we interpret the overall score and how sensitive it is to imbalances across dimensions. In the following section we depart from deta normalized via AMPI Constraied Min-Max and apply two different aggregation methods.

3.2 Additive Aggregation

The additive aggregation method computes the composite index as a weighted sum of the normalized indicators: \[I_i = \sum_{j=1}^{k} w_j z_{ij}\] Where: - \(I_i\) is the composite index score for country \(i\) - \(w_j\) is the weight assigned to indicator \(j\) (often set to 1/k for equal weighting) - \(z_{ij}\) is the normalized score of country \(i\) on indicator \(j\) This method assumes perfect substitutability between indicators: a low score on one indicator can be fully compensated by a high score on another. It is straightforward and easy to interpret, but it may not capture the multidimensional nature of development if we want to penalize imbalances across dimensions.

3.3 Power-Mean Aggregation

The power-mean aggregation method generalizes the arithmetic mean by introducing a parameter \(p\) that controls the degree of substitutability: \[I_i = \left(\frac{1}{k} \sum_{j=1}^{k} z_{ij}^p\right)^{1/p}\] Where: - \(I_i\) is the composite index score for country \(i\) - \(p\) is the power parameter that determines the aggregation behavior - \(z_{ij}\) is the normalized score of country \(i\) on indicator \(j\) When \(p = 1\), this reduces to the arithmetic mean (perfect substitutability). When \(p < 1\), it penalizes imbalance more strongly, and when \(p > 1\), it allows for more compensation between indicators. The choice of \(p\) reflects normative judgments about how we value balance across dimensions. When \(p \to 0\), the power mean approaches the geometric mean, which is a common method for composite indices that penalizes imbalance.

3.4 AMPI Aggregation: Arithmetic Mean + Penalty

The AMPI aggregation method combines the arithmetic mean of the normalized indicators with a penalty term based on the coefficient of variation (CV) time the standard deviation (σ) to account for imbalance across dimensions: \[I_i = \bar{z}_i - CV_i*\sigma\] This method rewards countries that perform well on average across all indicators while penalizing those with high variability (imbalance) across dimensions. The CV term captures the relative dispersion of a country’s performance across indicators, and the σ term scales the penalty based on overall variability in the dataset.

3.5 Minimum aggregation

The minimum aggregation method computes the composite index as the minimum of the normalized indicators: \[I_i = \min_{j=1}^{k} z_{ij}\] Where: - \(I_i\) is the composite index score for country \(i\) - \(z_{ij}\) is the normalized score of country \(i\) on indicator \(j\) This method assumes no substitutability between indicators: a low score on any one indicator determines the overall score. It emphasizes the importance of balanced performance across all dimensions, but it can be overly sensitive to outliers and may not reflect overall performance if one indicator is an extreme outlier.
